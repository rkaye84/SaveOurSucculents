Challenging Plants

Proceed with caution - these plants will only live happily with someone who knows their stuff when it comes to houseplanting.

If you do happen to be a relative expert however then please, continue.

English Ivy
Fiddle Leaf Fig
Orchid

Plenty of light. However careful not to leave somewhere excessively hot as can lead to drying and discolouration. Too cold an environment in contrast will lead to a purple discolouration.

Water: Like an evenly moist environment, and so freely water. Particularly during winter, they will need a little extra in the winter. Spray with soft water weekly. Can dilute with a quarter of fertiliser to improve performance.

Grow in a soil environment for optimum water retention and drainage.

Common pests affecting English Ivy plants are spider mites, aphids, mealybugs and scale insects. Red spider mites are often difficult to see without a close inspection. However, white webs formed on the plant are usually indicative of a spider mite infestation. Remove the infested leaves and treat the plant with a pesticide or insecticidal soap.

Remember the roots – as in origin. Fiddle leaf figs come from jungle environments: dark, dense, warm and wet.

To replicate the water level, think rainfall. The soil should be consistently damp, never soaking. To avoid overwatering, use this little trick from Chris Raimondi, president of the Raimondi Horticultural Group: to water properly, wait until the top inch of soil is dry. Then, thoroughly drench until water comes out the bottom of the pot and leave it to slowly dry out again. Signs of underwatering include limp, floppy leaves and foliage yellowing, browning then dropping off.

Light. This plant needs quite an average level of light – shouldn’t be in direct sunlight though not in the darkness so save the south facing windows for your succulents! Signs of incorrect light exposure include flagging foliage and pale, spotty, or wan-looking leaves – something to look out for when buying also.

Careful with the food – not much of an appetite so fertilise only once or twice a year.

Watch out for this temperature – at home in a hot jungle and will be fine at a normal, homely temperature however will falter quickly when exposed to the cold. Avoid placing in a draft eg from a door or window.

Orchids require ample water, but should certainly be left to dry out between waterings. To avoid overwatering, press a finger into the soil to test how damp it is. If it’s dry, give it some water, if not let it be – it’s happy!

Indoor orchids are fans of humid environments (50-70%) which you can increase in your home to make it comfortable by placing a tray of water or pebbles below the orchid, misting daily – or alternatively investing in a humidifier.

While producing new growth, orchids will need fertilising once to twice weekly. This need lessens as they mature, to only month then once they become dormant, stop although.

If your orchid appears to become unhappy (stop blooming), replotting may be necessary. This should be done every other year anyway, as orchids can outgrow their homes or the soil may become crumbly. To replot, pick a slightly larger pot (orchid containers have holes to increase air circulation). Put your orchid potting mix into a large bowl and cover with boiling water. Drain once it reaches room temperature. They are very sensitive when it comes to bacteria and germs. Make a solution of ½ cup of household bleach and 1 gallon of water. Soak the planter in this, as well as any tools you use. Wash your hands before you proceed. Gently pull the pot away from the plant and wash off the roots. Use sharp scissors to cut off any brown or rotting roots. Fill the new planter with the soaked potting medium and place the plant so that the base is right at the top of the medium. Use a chopstick to help push bits of planting medium in between the roots. Keep the orchid misted for at least a week until the new roots begin to appear.

Also keep an eye out for signs of pests or disease. Orchids are occasionally affected by mealybugs, scale, and aphids. These can usually be washed off or treated with insecticidal soap.
